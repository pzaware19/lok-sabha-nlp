What Does India’s Parliament Actually Talk About?

Mapping the ideological landscape of Indian democracy from 150,000 parliamentary questions using unsupervised machine learning. No labels, no hand-coding, just text.

Piyush Zaware

150,000 Questions

16th – 18th Lok Sabha (2014–2026)

13 Parties

LDA · STM · Word2Vec · UMAP

Every parliamentary session, Indian MPs submit hundreds of written questions to government ministries. These are not speeches or debates. They are targeted, on-the-record demands for information: what is the government doing about farmer suicides, what is the status of highway construction in Bihar, how many minorities were displaced last year. Taken together, across three Lok Sabhas and 13 parties, they form a detailed paper trail of what each party actually cares about.

This project mines the complete archive of starred (oral) questions from the 16th, 17th, and 18th Lok Sabha using unsupervised machine learning. The central question: can you recover the ideological map of Indian politics purely from how MPs phrase their questions, without any human annotation?

The answer is yes, and the results are sharper than expected.

The Data

150K

Total parliamentary questions across the 16th, 17th, and 18th Lok Sabha

13

Parties covered: BJP, INC, Left, BSP, AAP, TMC, SP, JDU, DMK, TDP, RJD, Shiv Sena, Regional

205

Documents in the corpus (one per party per parliamentary session)

The dataset is the opensansad/lok-sabha-qa corpus on HuggingFace: 150,629 questions covering the 16th (2014–2019), 17th (2019–2024), and 18th (2024–) Lok Sabha. The analysis focuses on starred questions, the oral variety submitted by MPs before each session. Starred questions are richer in political content than unstarred written submissions. A minister must be present to answer them in the House, which means parties use them strategically.

MP-to-party mapping was constructed by scraping Wikipedia’s constituency member lists for all three Lok Sabhas (three pages, automated, no API needed). This yielded a 38% match rate on starred questions, covering BJP, INC, and most major opposition parties with sufficient observations for reliable estimation.

After matching, the corpus was preprocessed into 205 party-session documents: one document per party per parliamentary session, treated as the unit of analysis. Each document is the concatenated text of all starred questions submitted by that party in that session.

Methods

Method What it does Key output
LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allocation) Discovers K recurring topics without supervision. Each document is a mixture of topics; each topic is a distribution over words. 15 topics, each party’s topic weights
STM (Structural Topic Model) Extends LDA by allowing party identity and Lok Sabha number to shift topic prevalence. Asks: does BJP talk about topic 7 more than INC, and did that change over time? Marginal effects of party on each topic
Word2Vec + Kozlowski (2019) Trains a 100-dimensional word embedding on all 150K questions. Recovers ideological dimensions as directions in embedding space using antonym pairs. Party scores on Hindutva, Left-Right, Rural-Urban axes
HAC (Hierarchical Agglomerative Clustering) Groups parties by cosine distance in TF-IDF space. No prespecified number of clusters. Dendrogram showing party linguistic families
GMM (Gaussian Mixture Model) Soft clustering of party-session documents in PCA space, with BIC-selected number of clusters. 6-cluster solution with probabilistic assignments
UMAP / t-SNE Non-linear dimensionality reduction from 3,258 dimensions to 2D for visualization. Visual map of the document corpus
Note

The Kozlowski method explained. An ideological dimension is a direction in word embedding space. The Hindutva dimension, for example, is the vector from the centroid of {secular, minority, mosque, constitution, equality} toward the centroid of {hindu, mandir, temple, cow, ram, ayodhya}. Each party’s position is computed as the TF-IDF-weighted average projection of their full vocabulary onto this direction. The method requires no labelled data. It recovers ideology purely from which words appear in similar contexts.

Source: Kozlowski, Taddy & Evans (2019), “The Geometry of Culture”, American Sociological Review.

Key Findings

1. The Ideological Map of Indian Parties

The most direct test of the method: do word embeddings recover a structure that resembles the known ideological landscape of Indian politics?

The structure matches what a political scientist would draw by hand. BJP and Shiv Sena sit at the Hindu-nationalist pole. Left parties (CPI, CPI-M) and AAP cluster at the secular end. INC occupies the centre-left, consistent with its broad-tent identity.

On the economic axis, the ordering is also informative. BSP and RJD use strongly redistributive, pro-poor language. BJP and TDP skew toward market, investment, and growth vocabulary. AAP sits in an unusual quadrant: secular but economically rightward, which aligns with its urban governance and anti-corruption brand rather than a classic left programme.

2. BJP’s Language Has Shifted Over a Decade in Government

The Hindutva score, BJP’s average position on the nationalist dimension, increases monotonically from the 16th to the 18th Lok Sabha.

The shift is not dramatic, but it is consistent and monotonic across all three Lok Sabhas. The language BJP MPs choose when questioning government ministries has moved measurably toward religious and civilisational vocabulary over a decade in power. This is observable without coding a single document by hand.

3. Topic Discovery: What Parliament Talks About

LDA identifies 15 latent topics from the corpus. The perplexity curve below shows how model fit improves with more topics; K=15 was selected as the elbow point before diminishing returns.

The top terms for each topic reveal coherent parliamentary themes.

The topics that emerge include: railway and transport infrastructure, agricultural welfare and farmer issues, sugar industry and pricing, judicial and electoral matters, public health and AIIMS, education and universities, water resources and river projects, petroleum and energy, defence and border security, and state-level budgetary allocations.

Topic distribution by party:

BJP concentrates on infrastructure, defence, and state-level development. INC focuses on governance, accountability, and judicial matters. Left parties show strong concentration on labour, wages, and public sector employment. Regional parties show sharp, idiosyncratic peaks consistent with their state-specific mandates: NCP on Maharashtra agriculture, DMK on Tamil Nadu water disputes and Dravidian cultural issues.

4. Structural Topic Model: Party Effects and Time Trends

The Structural Topic Model extends LDA by allowing party identity and Lok Sabha number to predict topic prevalence directly, estimating a marginal effect for each covariate on each topic.

The time trend plot shows which topics have grown or shrunk in parliamentary questioning across the three Lok Sabhas. The BJP-INC contrast plot shows the topics most differentially associated with each party, net of the overall topic distribution. Topics in the upper-left quadrant are distinctively BJP; those in the lower-right are distinctively INC.

5. The Document Map

Each of the 205 party-session documents projected from the full 3,258-term TF-IDF space into 2D. Two projection methods side by side: UMAP (preserves global structure) and t-SNE (emphasises local cluster separation).

Both methods agree on the broad structure. BJP and INC form the two largest and most central clusters, reflecting their dominant presence across all sessions. Left parties cluster tightly together. Regional parties scatter to the periphery, consistent with their narrow, state-specific focus. The two methods differ mainly in how compressed the regional party cloud appears, with t-SNE pulling the smaller parties further apart.

6. Clustering: Which Parties Use Similar Language?

Two clustering approaches applied to the same TF-IDF party vectors: hierarchical agglomerative clustering, which builds a dendrogram without requiring a prespecified number of clusters, and Gaussian mixture modelling in PCA space, which yields a soft probabilistic assignment.

The dendrogram reveals a clear structure: BJP and NDA-aligned parties (JDU, TDP) cluster together, reflecting shared government priorities. INC, SP, and BSP form a second cluster with a common welfare-oriented vocabulary. Left parties sit as a tight sub-cluster within the opposition grouping. Regional parties (TMC, DMK, AIADMK) form a separate branch.

The GMM solution with 6 clusters roughly tracks this structure but further separates the micro-regional parties that appear in only one Lok Sabha.

7. Ministry Focus: Where Each Party Directs Its Questions

Parliamentary questions are addressed to specific ministries. The heatmap below shows each party’s distribution of questions across ministries, revealing surveillance priorities.

BJP MPs question the Ministry of Railways and Road Transport heavily, consistent with the party’s infrastructure-first governance brand. INC concentrates on Finance, Home Affairs, and External Affairs, the domains of national accountability. Left parties direct disproportionate attention to Labour and Employment. Regional parties show ministry distributions that closely track their state’s economic profile: DMK on Textiles and Fisheries, TDP on Agriculture.

8. How Topics Have Evolved Over Time

Topic composition across parliamentary sessions, shown separately for BJP and INC to highlight how each party’s agenda has shifted.

BJP’s topic mix shifts noticeably between the 16th and 17th Lok Sabha, with infrastructure and agriculture topics expanding. INC’s mix is more stable but shows a gradual shift toward governance and institutional accountability topics, consistent with an opposition party trying to hold government to account on process rather than policy.

9. Semantic Neighbours of Key Political Terms

The word2vec embedding trained on 150K questions allows us to ask: what words appear in the most similar contexts to a given political term? The nearest neighbours reveal how concepts are linguistically entangled in Indian parliamentary discourse.

Some findings worth noting. “Farmer” sits close to drought, MSP, irrigation, and kisan, placing it firmly in a welfare-scarcity frame rather than an entrepreneurial one. “Terrorism” clusters with border, security, and Pakistan rather than with domestic extremism terms, indicating how the term is deployed in parliamentary questioning. “Caste” is proximate to reservation, OBC, and scheduled, pointing to its institutional rather than sociological usage in Parliament. “Corruption” sits close to scam, irregularity, and vigilance, reflecting the procedural register in which MPs raise accountability concerns.

Reproducing the Analysis

Requirements: R 4.3+. All packages install automatically.

source("code/_master.R")

The master file downloads all data from HuggingFace (no account needed), installs dependencies, and runs the full pipeline A1 through A8. Total runtime is roughly 15 minutes on a modern laptop. The word2vec training step is the most compute-intensive; it is cached after the first run.
