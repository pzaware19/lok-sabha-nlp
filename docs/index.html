What Does India’s Parliament Actually Talk About?

I ran unsupervised machine learning on 150,000 parliamentary questions from three Lok Sabhas. No labels, no hand-coding. Just the raw text of what Indian MPs ask the government, and what falls out when you let the data speak.

Piyush Zaware

150,000 Questions

16th – 18th Lok Sabha (2014–2026)

13 Parties

LDA · STM · Word2Vec · UMAP

BERTopic · VADER · spaCy NER

Starred questions are one of the more underused datasets in Indian political science. Every session, MPs submit written questions to ministries before the House sits. A minister has to be present to answer them. That matters, because it means parties use these questions deliberately. What is the government doing about farmer suicides? What is the status of the highway in Bihar? How many minorities were displaced? These are not speeches. They are on-the-record demands, filed by name, attached to a party.

I scraped the complete archive from HuggingFace, matched MPs to their parties via Wikipedia, and ran topic models, word embeddings, and clustering. The question I started with was simple: does the ideological structure of Indian politics show up in how parties phrase their questions? It does, pretty clearly.

The dataset is opensansad/lok-sabha-qa on HuggingFace, covering the 16th (2014–2019), 17th (2019–2024), and 18th (2024–) Lok Sabha. I focused on starred questions since they carry more political content. Unstarred questions are written submissions; starred ones require a minister to answer in the House, so parties pick them more carefully.

Party matching came from scraping Wikipedia’s constituency lists for all three Lok Sabhas. The match rate on starred questions was 38%, which gives enough coverage for BJP, INC, and most major opposition parties to work with reliably.

The unit of analysis is the party-session document: all starred questions submitted by a party in a given session, concatenated into one text. That gives 205 documents and a 3,258-term vocabulary after pruning.

Methods

Method What it does Output
LDA Finds recurring topics across all documents without supervision. Each document is a mixture; each topic is a set of words. 15 topics, party topic weights
STM Same as LDA but lets party identity and time shift topic prevalence. Asks whether BJP talks more about topic X than INC, and whether that changed. Marginal party and time effects per topic
Word2Vec + Kozlowski (2019) Trains word embeddings on 150K questions. Ideological dimensions are directions in this space, defined by antonym pairs. Party positions on Hindutva, Left-Right, Rural-Urban
HAC Hierarchical clustering of parties by vocabulary distance. No preset number of clusters. Dendrogram
GMM Soft clustering of party-session documents in PCA space. 6-cluster solution
UMAP / t-SNE Dimensionality reduction from 3,258 dimensions to 2D. Visual maps of the corpus
Note

On the word embedding approach. The idea from Kozlowski, Taddy & Evans (2019) is that you can encode an ideological dimension as a direction in word embedding space. The Hindutva dimension, for example, runs from words like {secular, minority, mosque, constitution} toward words like {hindu, mandir, temple, cow, ram, ayodhya}. A party’s position on this axis is just the average projection of its vocabulary onto that direction, weighted by TF-IDF. No human labelling involved.

Source: “The Geometry of Culture”, American Sociological Review, 2019.

Results

1. The Ideological Map

Start with the simplest question: does the word embedding space recover anything that looks like Indian political ideology?

It does. BJP and Shiv Sena are at the nationalist end. Left parties and AAP are at the secular end. INC is in the centre-left. The ordering you’d expect if you just asked a political scientist to draw it by hand is basically what comes out.

The economic axis has some surprises. BSP and RJD use the most redistributive, pro-poor language in Parliament. TDP, despite being a regional party, skews strongly toward market and investment vocabulary. AAP is the interesting case: it sits secular but economically rightward, which fits its governance-and-anti-corruption brand but puts it in an odd place relative to the traditional left.

2. BJP’s Language Has Shifted

BJP’s score on the Hindutva dimension goes up with each Lok Sabha.

It is not a dramatic jump. But it is monotonic across 16th, 17th, and 18th, which covers a decade in government. The vocabulary BJP MPs reach for when they question ministries has gradually shifted toward religious and civilisational terms. Whether you read that as ideological deepening or just a party mirroring its base is a separate question. The shift is there in the text either way.

3. What Parliament Actually Talks About

LDA finds 15 recurring topics. The curve below shows how model fit improves as you add more topics; 15 was chosen at the point where adding more stops helping much.

The topics that emerge are coherent and recognisable.

You get railway and highway infrastructure, farmer welfare and MSP, sugar industry regulation, judicial and electoral oversight, public health and AIIMS, higher education, water and river projects, petroleum pricing, border and defence, and state-level budget allocations. These are not surprising. They are the recurring preoccupations of Indian parliamentary life.

What is interesting is how unevenly they are distributed across parties.

BJP dominates infrastructure and defence topics. INC clusters around governance, accountability, and judicial matters, which makes sense for a party in opposition trying to question process rather than specific policies. Left parties are concentrated almost entirely on labour, wages, and public sector employment. Regional parties light up on narrow, state-specific topics: NCP on Maharashtra agriculture, DMK on water disputes and Dravidian cultural issues.

4. Party Effects and Time Trends

STM goes further than LDA by estimating how party identity and time shift topic prevalence directly.

The time trend shows which parliamentary concerns have grown and which have faded across Lok Sabhas. Some infrastructure topics expand over time; some governance topics contract. The BJP-INC contrast separates them cleanly on the dimensions you would expect: BJP on infrastructure and development, INC on accountability and oversight.

5. Mapping the Corpus

All 205 party-session documents projected from the 3,258-term TF-IDF space down to two dimensions. UMAP on the left preserves global structure; t-SNE on the right sharpens local clusters.

Both methods put BJP and INC at the centre as the two largest clouds. Left parties form a tight knot, which is what you would expect from parties that have a fairly consistent ideological line on what to ask about. Regional parties scatter outward, pulled by their state-specific vocabularies. The two projections agree on the broad picture but differ in how they treat the smaller parties: t-SNE stretches them further apart.

6. Which Parties Sound Like Each Other

Two clustering approaches on the same TF-IDF vectors: HAC builds a tree without requiring a preset number of groups, GMM fits soft clusters in PCA space.

The dendrogram groups BJP with JDU and TDP, which both governed alongside BJP and reflect shared infrastructure priorities in their questions. INC, SP, and BSP cluster together with a shared welfare-oriented vocabulary. Left parties form their own tight sub-cluster. Regional parties (TMC, DMK) branch off separately.

7. Which Ministries Get Questioned

Questions are addressed to specific ministries. This shows where each party directs its scrutiny.

BJP MPs question Railways and Road Transport heavily. INC focuses on Finance, Home Affairs, and External Affairs. Left parties concentrate on Labour and Employment almost exclusively relative to their size. Regional parties mirror their states: DMK on Textiles and Fisheries, TDP on Agriculture, NCP on Rural Development.

8. Topic Evolution Over Time

How have the topics each party raises shifted across sessions?

BJP’s mix shifts between the 16th and 17th Lok Sabha, with infrastructure and agriculture expanding. INC’s mix is more stable but tilts gradually toward governance and accountability topics. An opposition party in India has limited levers; questioning government process on Finance and Home Affairs is one of them.

9. What Words Live Near Each Other

The embedding lets you ask: what words appear in the most similar contexts to a given term? This is a rough map of how concepts cluster in parliamentary language.

A few things stand out. “Farmer” sits near drought, MSP, and irrigation. It is a scarcity and welfare word in Parliament, not an entrepreneurial one. “Terrorism” clusters with border, security, and Pakistan, which says something about how the term gets deployed in questions versus how it is discussed in civil society. “Caste” is proximate to reservation, OBC, and scheduled: institutional usage rather than sociological. “Corruption” lands near scam, irregularity, and vigilance. MPs raise it in procedural terms.

10. Summary

BERTopic: Topics Without the Bag-of-Words Assumption

LDA treats each document as a bag of tokens. It doesn’t know that “railway” and “train” are related, or that “farmer suicide” is a compound concept. BERTopic uses sentence embeddings instead — each question is projected into a 384-dimensional semantic space using a multilingual transformer, then HDBSCAN finds clusters of semantically similar questions, and c-TF-IDF extracts the distinctive terms per cluster.

Running BERTopic on all 11,841 starred questions (not the party-session aggregates) finds 29 coherent topics. About 25% of questions land in the outlier cluster — questions that don’t fit neatly into any topic. That’s not a failure; it’s honest. LDA forces every document into every topic with some weight; BERTopic refuses to.

The topics that emerge are sharper than LDA’s. Railway infrastructure, power and energy, MGNREGS and rural employment, pharmaceutical drugs, banking and loans, forest and pollution, sugar mills, coastal fisheries, AIIMS and hospitals — each topic has a clean semantic core. The LDA equivalent mixes these because the token “wise” and “proposed” appear everywhere and pollute topic boundaries.

The two methods agree on the broad thematic structure of parliamentary questioning but disagree in resolution. LDA gives 15 coarse topics with noisy term lists. BERTopic gives 29 finer topics with cleaner terms — at the cost of a 25% outlier rate and higher computational time.

Ministry Targeting

Every starred question is addressed to a specific ministry. That’s a second signal on top of the text — not just what parties talk about, but who they hold accountable. The analysis here asks whether parties target ministries strategically, or whether the distribution of questions across ministries roughly mirrors each party’s overall question volume.

The baseline is proportional questioning: if BJP asks 40% of all questions and Defence gets 5% of all questions, then BJP asking 2% of its questions to Defence is expected. The Pearson residual measures the deviation from that null.

A few things stand out. INC concentrates disproportionately on Finance and Home Affairs — the two ministries most exposed to accountability questions about economic policy and internal security. Left parties over-question Labour and Employment by a substantial margin. Regional parties target niche ministries tied to their state economies: Textiles, Fisheries, Steel.

Ministry concentration

Some parties spread their questions across many ministries. Others concentrate fire. The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (sum of squared ministry shares) measures this — higher means more concentrated.

DMK and the Left are the most concentrated — both have narrow, consistent mandates. BJP and Regional are the most diffuse, which makes sense: BJP has a broad governing agenda and regional parties have heterogeneous state-specific priorities.

How targeting has shifted over time

BJP’s targeting of Defence and Home Affairs grows across Lok Sabhas, consistent with an expanding security and sovereignty agenda. INC’s concentration on Finance increases in the 17th and 18th LS — as the opposition, it tracks economic policy failures more intensively when the government’s fiscal record becomes more contestable.

Tone and Adversarialism

Parliamentary questions have a formal grammar: “Will the Minister of X please state whether…” That structure is mandatory. But within it, the choice of framing differs. Has the government failed to protect minorities? is adversarial. What schemes has the government launched to promote farmer welfare? is aspirational. VADER extracts this signal by scoring the compound sentiment of each question’s text.

The overall distribution is positive — parliamentary questions are written to get answers, not to insult. But the relative differences across parties are informative.

Left parties are the most adversarial, with 25% of their starred questions classified as negative in tone. INC follows at 16%, slightly above BJP at 13%. This is consistent with the incumbency gradient — opposition parties have more reason to frame questions as government failures — but the Left’s high rate is notable even adjusting for their perpetual opposition status across all three Lok Sabhas.

Do adversarialism levels change over time?

The temporal pattern shows a widening gap. Opposition parties — particularly INC and the Left — become more adversarial across Lok Sabhas, while BJP’s tone stays relatively stable. Whether this reflects deteriorating political relations, a more combative opposition strategy, or the changing nature of issues in Parliament is not identified here.

Which ministries attract the most critical questioning?

Home Affairs and Finance attract the most adversarial questioning. That tracks the ministry-targeting analysis above: these are the ministries INC and the Left concentrate on, and they concentrate there because the questions they ask about security and fiscal policy are inherently more confrontational.

Which topics attract adversarial framing?

The topic-level view maps cleanly onto the subject matter. Topics about judicial matters, security incidents, and minority welfare attract the most adversarial framing. Topics about infrastructure schemes, tourism, and sports attract almost none.

Note

VADER caveat. VADER was calibrated on social media and news text, not formal parliamentary English. The parliamentary register inflates positive scores because declarative, neutral language reads as “not negative.” Absolute score levels are not meaningful — the within-sample variation across parties and ministries is.

Geographic Focus: Where Parties Look

Every question comes with text that mentions specific places. spaCy’s NER pipeline (en_core_web_sm) extracts the states named in each question. This lets us ask: do regional parties really concentrate their parliamentary activity on their home states? And how does BJP’s geographic spread as a national governing party compare?

The answer is stark.

Regional parties are entirely concentrated. Left mentions only Kerala. TDP mentions only Telangana and Andhra Pradesh. DMK’s state mentions go almost entirely to Tamil Nadu-adjacent issues. JDU’s geographic focus on Bihar is the highest HHI among parties with substantial question volume.

BJP is the most diffuse. As the national governing party, its MPs raise questions touching 14 different states. No single state accounts for more than 15% of its geographic mentions.

Who gets named?

Person naming follows a pattern: government parties name bureaucrats and scheme beneficiaries, opposition parties name politicians they are holding accountable. The specific individuals vary but the structural difference is consistent.

What the Results Do Not Tell You

The incumbency confound

Parliamentary questions are not a pure record of ideology. They are also a record of political position. A party in government asks: is the highway project on schedule? Is fertiliser subsidy reaching farmers? A party in opposition asks: why is the auditor’s report suppressed? Why are minorities being targeted? These are structurally different questions, and the difference is positional, not necessarily ideological.

The figure below documents this directly. Across the 205 party-session documents, NDA government parties systematically emphasise different topics than opposition parties, independent of any ideological interpretation.

Why this cannot be fixed with the available data

The natural response is to partial out incumbency status and see what remains. The problem is that this only works if some parties switch sides across the sample period, giving within-party variation to identify the incumbency effect separately from ideology. In this dataset, BJP is in government for every Lok Sabha observed, and INC is in opposition for every Lok Sabha observed. So incumbency status is perfectly collinear with party identity for the two parties the comparison hinges on. Regressing out in_gov does not remove the incumbency effect — it removes the BJP-INC contrast itself, which is circular.

The only genuine switchers are TDP (NDA in 16th LS, opposition in 17th, NDA again in 18th) and JDU (opposition in 16th, NDA in 17th and 18th). TDP won only 3 Lok Sabha seats in 2019, so its 17th LS corpus is too sparse. That leaves JDU as the primary source of within-party variation — not enough to credibly identify the incumbency effect across 13 parties.

The STM with incumbency added as a covariate (~ bjp + inc + left + lok_num + session + in_gov) does not solve this either. When party indicators and in_gov are jointly included in the prevalence formula, the model faces the same multicollinearity. The in_gov coefficient is identified mainly from the three switchers.

What follows from this

The main findings should be read as descriptive text differences, not identified ideological positions. The claim that BJP MPs and Left MPs write differently is well-supported — they clearly do. Whether that difference reflects ideology, government responsibilities, or both is not separable with this research design.

Proper identification would require an instrument for coalition entry (something that predicts which parties join NDA but is uncorrelated with their vocabulary independent of incumbency) or a regression discontinuity around close elections that determine coalition composition. Neither is feasible here.

The BJP trajectory finding (Hindutva score rising monotonically across three Lok Sabhas) faces the additional problem of having only three data points, and the political context changed sharply between them — Ayodhya verdict, CAA, Article 370 revocation all fall between the 16th and 17th LS. The text shift is real; whether it is ideological deepening or a contextual response is not identified.
